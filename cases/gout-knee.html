Case 1 – Gout in the Knee (Easy)

Region: Knee · Pathology: Crystal Arthropathy · Level: Intro

38-year-old male presenting with a 3-week history of left knee pain, swelling, warmth, and reduced range of motion.

Clinical vignette

The patient first injured his left knee while running 10 years ago and nearly recovered with physiotherapy. Over the past 5 years, he has had intermittent episodes of knee pain and swelling occurring every few months, sometimes triggered by camping, increased alcohol intake, or red meat. He has never experienced classic podagra.

Over the past 3 weeks, he developed an acute flare with warmth, swelling, and limited range of motion due to pain and effusion, improving partially with celecoxib. He is otherwise well with no inflammatory back pain, dactylitis, uveitis, or constitutional symptoms.

Question 1

What symptoms would you inquire about given the most likely diagnosis?

  • History of psoriasis
  • History of preceding gastrointestinal or genitourinary infections
  • Family history of gout
  • History of Raynaud’s phenomenon
Explanation

Psoriasis increases the risk of gout and raises consideration of psoriatic arthritis. Preceding infections are relevant when evaluating reactive arthritis in young males with oligoarthritis. A family history of gout supports the diagnosis. Raynaud’s phenomenon is less relevant here.

Bedside ultrasound

Ultrasound of the bilateral knees demonstrates effusions and a hyperechoic line along the articular cartilage surface.

(Images would be placed here: lateral femorotibial joint line and suprapatellar views.)

Question 2

What is the finding indicated by the yellow arrow?

  • Interface sign (normal artifact)
  • Chondrosis
  • Double contour sign
  • Pseudo–double contour sign
Answer

Double contour sign — representing monosodium urate crystal deposition on the surface of articular cartilage.

Question 3

Which investigations are most helpful?

  • Serum urate level
  • Synovial fluid analysis
  • Dual-energy CT (if needed)
Explanation

RF, anti-CCP, and HLA-B*27 are not confirmatory here. MRI may show synovitis but is inferior to DECT for gout. Arthrocentesis remains diagnostic.

Final diagnosis

Reveal

Oligoarticular gout flare

Supported by dietary triggers, elevated serum urate, ultrasound double contour sign, and negatively birefringent monosodium urate crystals on synovial fluid analysis.
